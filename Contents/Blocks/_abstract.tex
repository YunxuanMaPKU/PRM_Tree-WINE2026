\begin{abstract}
Markets for data promise to unlock its economic value, but in practice they see surprisingly little activity---chiefly because those who supply data are not adequately rewarded for doing so. A defining trait of data is that it can be copied: a buyer may turn purchased data into a new product and sell it onward, so one source seeds a whole cascade of downstream deals. An upstream seller, earning nothing from these later deals, is in turn reluctant to sell at all. Prior work alleviates this through \emph{profit reallocation}, which returns part of downstream revenue to upstream sellers, but existing analyses assume resales form a chain---each seller serves a single buyer---whereas in practice one data product is typically sold to many buyers, producing a \emph{tree} of transactions.

We study profit reallocation on tree-structured data markets. We introduce a sequential, tree-based trading game and a general, budget-feasible profit reallocation mechanism on it, and we develop efficient algorithms to compute the induced equilibria: a polynomial-time exact algorithm for discrete valuations and a fully polynomial-time approximation scheme (FPTAS) for continuous valuations. We then prove that, under mild assumptions, any budget-feasible reallocation weakly expands the trades that occur in equilibrium, and any budget-balanced reallocation additionally weakly improves social welfare, relative to a baseline that reallocates nothing. Experiments on synthetic markets confirm that the benefits are substantial and persist even when the assumptions fail, and that they grow with the depth and branching of the tree.

\end{abstract}
